## When useful

Use this note when the business sells physical goods to consumers — fashion,
homeware, gifts, electronics, supplies. It gives the Strategy step
sector-specific starting assumptions to combine with situation diagnosis
rather than treating retail as a generic "small business."

## Poor-fit conditions

A poor fit for pure services, food & beverage (use the hospitality note),
and for very large multi-branch retail chains that operate more like a
brand than an SME — the dynamics here assume owner-scale operation.

## Concrete sector-specific observations

1. **Seasonality dominates planning.** retail's biggest windows in Egypt
   are Ramadan, Eids, back-to-school, and winter gifting; a 12-week plan
   that ignores these windows misses most of the year's revenue opportunity
   and a plan that overlaps all of them at once cannot attribute wins.
2. **Stock and pricing transparency convert.** clear photos, clear prices,
   and "in stock / out of stock" honesty are the conversion levers; vague
   "DM for price" is a conversion killer for new customers, even though
   some owners prefer it.
3. **Discovery is increasingly off-platform first.** many retail customers
   find items on Instagram/TikTok and then go to the seller's WhatsApp —
   the WhatsApp reply speed and order path are the real conversion engine,
   not the post itself.
4. **Repeat depends on category and offer.** fashion and gifts often won't
   repeat monthly; homeware/consumables will. Retention efforts should
   match the real repeat cadence of the category, not a generic
   newsletter.
5. **Returns and trust.** clarity on returns/exchanges and real photos
   (not stock) materially reduces complaints and improves review
   velocity, which feeds discovery — trust is a growth input for retail,
   not a cost.

## Explicit limitation

This is a starting point, not a complete retail strategy. Combine with
situation diagnosis, STP, the chosen objective, and the relevant channel
playbooks before producing a plan.
