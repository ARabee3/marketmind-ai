## When to use it

Use 5Cs + SWOT at the very start of a strategy engagement, before picking an
objective or a channel. It is the diagnosis step: if the owner cannot state
their company's real position, customers, and competitors in a few
sentences, any objective chosen next is a guess. This is also the right
framework when an owner says "I just need more sales" but cannot say who
buys, who competes, or why repeat customers return.

## How to apply it to a small SME

Run the 5Cs as a short interview, not a research project. **Company**: what
do you sell, and what is the one thing you do better than a direct
alternative in the owner's own words? **Customers**: who actually buys today
(one persona, not five), and what pushed the last three paying customers to
choose you? **Competitors**: name two-to-three real competitors the customer
would use if you closed tomorrow, and one thing each does better than you.
**Collaborators**: suppliers, delivery platforms, or partners whose decisions
constrain or enable you (e.g. a single delivery platform that drives most
orders). **Context**: one external pressure that matters now — seasonality,
a new regulator, a platform policy change, a tax rule.

Then collapse the five answers into a four-cell SWOT. Keep it to the four
strongest items per cell, not a brainstorm dump. The point is a decision-
ready picture: the most useful output is the diagonal — using a Strength to
seize an Opportunity, or a Weakness that threatens the business if a Threat
lands. That diagonal becomes the seed of the objective chosen next.

## Common misapplication for solo-owner businesses

The usual failure is scope inflation: a solo owner treats every cell as
exhaustive and produces a 40-item SWOT nobody acts on. A second failure is
disguising opinions as facts — listing "great food" as a Strength when no
customer or review actually says so. A third is ignoring Collaborators and
Context because they feel external; for an SME on a delivery platform, the
platform's commission and policy changes are usually the dominant Context
item and must be named, not skipped. Keep each cell to the few items the
owner can defend with evidence (a customer quote, a review, a sales line).

## How it feeds the next Strategy step

The SWOT diagonal and the named customer/competitor become the inputs to
objective selection (`smart-objectives-funnel-mapping`) and positioning
(`audience-positioning-stp`). The Strategy step reads the situations
diagnosis and asks: given this Strength, this customer, and this Threat,
which of the five objectives (awareness, acquisition, conversion, retention,
launch) is actually the right one for the next 12 weeks? A diagnosis that
names a real customer and a real competitor makes the next step
evidence-anchored; a vague diagnosis makes the next step a guess.
