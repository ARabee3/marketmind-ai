## When to use it

Use this framework after situation diagnosis and STP, before channel
selection. It is the step that stops an owner from running awareness ads
when the real bottleneck is that website visitors don't convert, or running
acquisition campaigns when the cheapest win is repeat purchases from
existing customers. If the owner cannot name the one metric a plan is
trying to move, the plan is not a plan.

## How to apply it to a small SME

Pick exactly one of the five canonical objectives — awareness, acquisition,
conversion, retention, launch — and write it as a SMART goal: Specific
(one audience, one behaviour), Measurable (one number with a baseline),
Achievable (fits the team's real capacity and budget), Relevant (actually
moves the business), Time-bound (a 12-week window, not "ongoing"). Then map
that objective to one funnel stage so measurement is honest: awareness maps
to reach and recall; acquisition maps to new-customer actions (first
order, first booking); conversion maps to turning interested traffic into
paying customers (website visit → completed order); retention maps to
repeat behaviour and advocacy maps to referrals and reviews.

The discipline is one objective, one metric, one funnel stage, one
window. A 12-week plan that tries to move awareness and conversion and
retention simultaneously measures nothing well, because no single metric can
be attributed cleanly across all three. If the owner genuinely has two
objectives, pick the bottleneck for this 12 weeks and defer the second.

## Common misapplication for solo-owner businesses

The first failure is a non-measurable goal ("build brand awareness") with no
baseline number and no end date — this is a wish, not an objective. The
second is choosing an objective that does not match the bottleneck: running
awareness ads to push more traffic at a website that already gets visits but
does not convert, when conversion is the real problem. The third is
choosing retention with no existing customer list to retain from. The fourth
is "Achievable" ignored: a solo owner with a 3,000 EGP monthly budget
targeting a million-person reach has set a goal the resources cannot
support, which guarantees the plan fails for capacity reasons, not strategy
reasons.

## How it feeds the next Strategy step

The chosen SMART objective and its funnel stage become the spine of the
plan: they determine which channel playbooks are relevant, which content
pillars matter, which budget mode is appropriate, and which KPI the
measurement plan must instrument. The objective also determines which
knowledge entries the Strategy step retrieves — e.g. a retention objective
pulls the retention objective playbook and the retention-relevant channel
notes, not acquisition entries. A crisp SMART objective is what makes the
rest of the plan coherent and reviewable.
