## When to use it

Use this framework after STP has selected a target customer and before the
Strategy Agent chooses channels, messages, or offers. It answers three
linked questions: what customer needs matter now, what value proposition the
business can credibly defend, and which relevant 7Ps checks could change the
plan. It is useful when the owner has a target audience but the offer still
sounds generic, when the message lists features instead of customer outcomes,
or when operational details may make the promise hard to deliver.

## How to apply it to a small SME

Start with the customer needs already visible in discovery: the job the
customer wants done, the pain that blocks purchase, the desired gain, and
the evidence the owner has for that need. Keep only needs the business can
serve with its current product, team, and operating constraints. A need that
is attractive but unsupported by delivery capacity is a poor fit for the next
Strategy step.

Turn the chosen need into a value proposition in plain language: for this
customer, the business helps with this need, through this offer or service
experience, because this proof makes the promise believable. The proof should
be something the owner can show or explain: a process, guarantee policy,
response time, service standard, location advantage, staff expertise, or
consistent customer feedback. If the proof is only an adjective, the value
proposition is not ready.

Then run only the relevant 7Ps checks. **Product** asks whether the offer
actually solves the chosen need. **Price** asks whether the value and payment
terms support the promise. **Place** asks whether the customer can buy or
access it with low friction. **Promotion** asks whether the message says the
need, promise, and proof clearly. **People** asks whether staff behaviour can
deliver the promise. **Process** asks whether ordering, booking, delivery, or
support creates friction. **Physical evidence** asks whether visible proof
such as reviews, photos, packaging, signage, receipts, or onboarding material
supports trust. Skip any P that cannot change the decision.

## Common misapplication for solo-owner businesses

The usual failure is writing a slogan before choosing a customer need. A
second failure is copying every P into the plan even when most of them do
not affect the decision. A third is promising a value proposition the team
cannot deliver in busy periods. For a small team, a narrow promise that can
be fulfilled is stronger than a broad promise that depends on perfect
execution.

## How it feeds the next Strategy step

The output is a short decision brief: target need, value proposition, proof,
poor fit warnings, and the relevant 7Ps checks that should shape the plan.
The Strategy Agent uses that brief to choose channels, content pillars,
offers, and measurement without drifting into generic marketing advice. If a
check exposes a delivery gap, the next Strategy step should either narrow the
promise or treat the gap as an owner decision before planning campaigns.
