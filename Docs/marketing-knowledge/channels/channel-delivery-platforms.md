## Use cases (fit)

Being listed on a third-party delivery platform (e.g. a food delivery app)
is useful for any food business whose customers already order that way and
would otherwise order from a competitor — it is mostly a discovery and
conversion channel, not an awareness one. It fits QSRs, cafés, bakeries,
and any kitchen that can absorb delivery orders without breakeven erosion
after platform commission.

## Poor-fit conditions

Delivery platforms are a poor fit when the per-order margin after
commission is negative or near-zero and the owner has no plan to drive
repeat orders off-platform — that turns the platform into a loss channel.
They are also a poor fit when the kitchen cannot keep up with peak delivery
demand and quality drops, or when the business is a dine-in experience that
does not travel (some food does not deliver well). They can also be a poor
fit when the business depends on a single platform whose commission or
policy changes can wipe out the model — concentration risk.

## Required assets

A listed menu with accurate prices and photos, a reliable packaging setup,
capacity to fulfill delivery orders at the agreed time, and a
platform-permitted opt-in path for owned follow-up. Do not reuse a phone
number obtained only for order fulfilment. Any marketing contact must use
separate consent that states the marketing purpose, sender identity, and an
easy opt-out or withdrawal route.

## Team effort / capacity implications

Fulfillment is the main capacity cost — the kitchen side, packaging, and
timing discipline. Listing itself is low effort but maintaining the menu
(sold-out items, price changes) is a recurring small task. For a solo
owner, one platform done well beats three platforms done poorly.

## Organic vs. paid role

There is little "organic vs. paid" in the social sense; the relevant levers
are placement (ranking inside the platform, sometimes boosted by platform
ads), reviews, and operational metrics (acceptance rate, fulfillment
time). The owner's paid decision is platform-internal promotion, not social
ad spend.

## Budget notes

`organic_only` here means listing and operating well without platform
promotion. `monthly_amount` may mean platform-internal promotion budget,
which competes with social ad budget — be deliberate about which. The key
unit-economics check is margin per order after commission, not gross order
count.

## Measurement

Measure weekly order volume, average order value, margin per order after
commission, fulfillment metrics (acceptance rate, on-time), and the count
of platform-permitted opt-ins from delivery customers. The most misleading
metric is gross order volume growing while per-order margin is negative — a
growing loss is not success. Track unit economics, not just volume.

## Risks

The principal risks are margin erosion through commission, depending on a
single platform (concentration), and treating fulfilment contact data as a
marketing list. Delivery-platform data use must stay within platform terms
and local consent law; build owned channels only through explicit opt-in
with a clear marketing purpose, sender identity, and opt-out/withdrawal
path. A third risk is operational quality dropping under delivery volume,
which harms the dine-in brand too.
