## Fit signals (when this objective is right now)

Launch is the right priority when something genuinely new is being
introduced to market: a brand-new business opening, a new location, a new
product line, a new menu, a new service, or a relaunch after a closure or
rebrand. It is a composite objective — it combines a short awareness spike
with an immediate conversion goal — because a launch that creates awareness
but no first customers is not a successful launch.

## Poor-fit signals

Launch is a poor fit for an established business with no new offering — that
is an awareness or acquisition problem dressed up as a launch. It is also a
poor fit when the launch date is not fixed and capacity to serve the
opening is not ready; a launch campaign that drives people to a business
that is not ready to take their money causes first-impression damage that is
expensive to undo. Launch is also a poor fit when the owner cannot define
what "the launch" actually is (a date, an offer, an opening) — without
that, there is nothing to measure success against.

## Typical funnel stage(s)

Launch spans awareness (announcing the new thing to the right people at the
right time) and conversion (turning launch attention into first customers in
a defined window). It is stage-mixed by definition.

## What a 12-week plan under this objective usually prioritizes

A 12-week launch plan usually prioritizes: (1) a fixed launch date and a
defined "launch offer" or reason to act in the launch window; (2) a
pre-launch build (teaser content, a complete profile, ready hours and a
working order path) so momentum isn't wasted on a broken funnel; (3) a
concentrated awareness spike immediately around the date on the channels
where the target segment is reachable; (4) immediate conversion capture so
first-day customers can be counted. The KPIs are launch-window reach (was
the right audience aware) and first-week new customers (did it convert),
measured against the launch date, not against an ongoing baseline.

## Risks of choosing this objective when it doesn't fit

Choosing launch when nothing is actually new spends the energy of a launch
on nothing in particular. Launching before capacity is ready produces a bad
first day that harms the entire subsequent conversion window. Treating a
launch as awareness-only (no first-customer goal) lets a launch "succeed" on
reach while failing to bring in any business. Treating a launch as a one-day
event with no follow-through wastes the awareness spike; the follow-through
window should be sized to outcome volume, spend, and the sales cycle rather
than fixed by default.
