## Fit signals (when this objective is right now)

Acquisition is the right priority when the business already has presence
and reach but too few new customers: the name is known locally and traffic
exists, but the count of first-time paying customers is the bottleneck.
Concrete signals: an established business that has plateaued and needs new
customers to grow; a business running only to existing followers and never
capturing people who discovered them; a seasonal business that needs to win
first-time customers before a peak (e.g. before Ramadan for a bakery).

## Poor-fit signals

Acquisition is a poor fit when the business cannot retain the customers it
already gets — the cheaper win is usually retention, not new acquisition.
It is also a poor fit when there is no measurable "first-time customer
action" defined (no first order, no first booking, no clear new-customer
signal in the point-of-sale or order system), because without that signal
the objective cannot be measured and therefore cannot be managed. Acquisition
is also a poor fit when there is no capacity to absorb new customers without
hurting the experience of existing ones.

## Typical funnel stage(s)

Acquisition spans the consideration-to-conversion band: it owns the count of
new customers who complete a first paid action, typically measured as new
customers per week and cost per new customer when paid is involved.

## What a 12-week plan under this objective usually prioritizes

A 12-week acquisition plan usually prioritizes: (1) defining and
instrumenting the "first paid action" so new customers can actually be
counted; (2) making it easy for interested people to convert — a working
phone/WhatsApp line, clear hours, an obvious address, a simple order path;
(3) a targeted offer for first-time customers (a reason to try now, not
"come eventually"); (4) a small, measurable acquisition channel where the
target segment is reachable (often a geotargeted Facebook/Instagram effort
or a Google Business Profile that converts local search). The KPI is new
customers per week and, where paid is used, cost per new customer.

## Risks of choosing this objective when it doesn't fit

Choosing acquisition when retention is the real bottleneck spends budget
replacing customers who leave instead of fixing why they leave. Choosing
acquisition with no first-customer signal defined produces an unmeasurable
plan. Choosing acquisition with an aggressive paid push before the
conversion path itself works (busy phone, unclear hours) burns money
sending people into a broken funnel.
